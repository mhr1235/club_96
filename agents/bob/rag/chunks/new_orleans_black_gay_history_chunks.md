## Chunk: new_orleans_bar_gatekeeping

Source: I'm From Driftwood, "Black & Gay In New Orleans From The 60s To Today" by Michael Hickerson
URL: https://imfromdriftwood.com/story/black-gay-in-new-orleans-from-the-60s-to-today-you-get-up-you-dust-yourself-off-and-you-fight-on/
Tags: New Orleans, Black gay history, bar access, gatekeeping, racism, nightlife

Michael Hickerson describes how Black gay men in 1970s New Orleans faced exclusion from predominantly white gay bars through unequal ID expectations, slow service, and door practices that made entry easier for white patrons than for Black patrons. The story shows that nightlife access can be shaped by informal gatekeeping, not only official policy.

Significance for Bob:
Club 96 outreach and venue operations should watch for subtle access barriers, including door policy, ID expectations, staff behavior, service order, and who feels safe entering the space.

## Chunk: new_orleans_community_persistence

Source: I'm From Driftwood, "Black & Gay In New Orleans From The 60s To Today" by Michael Hickerson
URL: https://imfromdriftwood.com/story/black-gay-in-new-orleans-from-the-60s-to-today-you-get-up-you-dust-yourself-off-and-you-fight-on/
Tags: New Orleans, Black gay history, resilience, community memory, intergenerational knowledge

Michael Hickerson frames Black gay life in New Orleans as a history of endurance, adaptation, and continued struggle across changing eras. His account connects childhood racial displacement, discovery of gay social spaces, bar exclusion, organizing, HIV/AIDS work, and post-Katrina survival.

Significance for Bob:
When Bob discusses community history or outreach, he can treat resilience as practical knowledge. Club 96 should not romanticize struggle, but should recognize that community survival often depends on relationships, memory, access, and persistent mutual support.

## Chunk: new_orleans_krewes_and_belonging

Source: I'm From Driftwood, "Black & Gay In New Orleans From The 60s To Today" by Michael Hickerson
URL: https://imfromdriftwood.com/story/black-gay-in-new-orleans-from-the-60s-to-today-you-get-up-you-dust-yourself-off-and-you-fight-on/
Tags: New Orleans, Mardi Gras krewes, Black gay history, belonging, cultural organizations, exclusion

Michael Hickerson recalls encountering gay Mardi Gras krewes and noticing that Black participation was limited or resisted in some organizations. His experiences with krewes show that cultural institutions can appear celebratory while still reproducing racial exclusion through membership, cost, custom, and social acceptance.

Significance for Bob:
Club 96 programming should ask who can participate, who can afford to participate, and who is treated as belonging. A glamorous event can still exclude people if membership, cost, or social norms make entry difficult.

## Chunk: new_orleans_hiv_service_work

Source: I'm From Driftwood, "Black & Gay In New Orleans From The 60s To Today" by Michael Hickerson
URL: https://imfromdriftwood.com/story/black-gay-in-new-orleans-from-the-60s-to-today-you-get-up-you-dust-yourself-off-and-you-fight-on/
Tags: New Orleans, HIV/AIDS, Black gay history, service work, mutual aid, health outreach

Michael Hickerson describes moving from nightlife and community visibility into HIV/AIDS service work, including housing and support services for people affected by HIV. His account connects social popularity, trust, and lived experience to the ability to reach people who might not be served by formal institutions alone.

Significance for Bob:
Club 96 should treat outreach as relationship-based work. Health, housing, and care needs may surface through nightlife and cultural programming, so Bob should think about partnerships, referrals, and trust-building alongside events.

## Chunk: new_orleans_katrina_service_disruption

Source: I'm From Driftwood, "Black & Gay In New Orleans From The 60s To Today" by Michael Hickerson
URL: https://imfromdriftwood.com/story/black-gay-in-new-orleans-from-the-60s-to-today-you-get-up-you-dust-yourself-off-and-you-fight-on/
Tags: New Orleans, Hurricane Katrina, HIV/AIDS services, displacement, Black communities, crisis planning

Michael Hickerson describes Hurricane Katrina as a disaster that disrupted HIV services, displaced clients, and intensified existing inequities for poor and Black communities in New Orleans. The story shows how fragile community support systems can become during crisis, especially when people are separated from familiar service networks.

Significance for Bob:
Club 96 should think about continuity, emergency planning, and resource networks. A community venue can become more valuable when it knows how to direct people toward help during disruption, displacement, or public health crisis.

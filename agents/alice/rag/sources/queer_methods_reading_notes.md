## Summary

Qwo-Li Driskill's "Doubleweaving Two-Spirit Critiques" argues that Two-Spirit critiques are rooted in Native histories, tribally specific knowledge, decolonial struggle, art, activism, land, community, and sovereignty. The essay challenges queer studies to remember whose land it is built on and to treat Native and Two-Spirit resistance as central rather than marginal. Driskill uses Cherokee doubleweaving as a method: distinct strands can be woven into a durable whole without becoming the same strand.

Grace Kyungwon Hong and Roderick A. Ferguson's introduction to "Strange Affinities" develops women of color feminism and queer of color critique as comparative methods for thinking coalition through difference, rather than through sameness or simple identity categories. It asks how racialized, gendered, and sexualized differences are produced, valued, devalued, and used to organize power.

## How Alice Might Use This

- Alice can reference "doubleweaving" when thinking about programming that holds multiple histories, audiences, and aesthetics together without flattening them.
- Alice can reference "strange affinities" when imagining coalitions that do not require everyone to be the same.
- Alice can use these readings to make the bookstore/bar more accountable: ask whose land, whose archive, whose bodies, whose labor, and whose aesthetics are being centered.
